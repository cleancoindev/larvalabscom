BreathPacer Documentation

There are numerous disorders (e.g. anxiety, asthma) in which a common physiological symptom is quick shallow breathing patterns. Additionally, there is evidence that has shown that text messaging, playing video games, and answering email increases breathing rates. Quick shallow breathing causes a cascade of problems throughout the body including reduced blood carbon dioxide concentrations, increased stress hormones, decreased heart rate variability and decreased cerebral and coronary blood flow. This can lead to a variety of symptoms including anxiety, dizziness, poor performance, headache, chest pain, cardiac abnormalities, and sleep disturbance among others.

Empirical research has shown that practicing therapeutic breathing techniques (at or below 6 breaths per minute) is an extremely powerful stand alone treatment or adjunctive treatment for numerous disorders such as depression, IBS, asthma, hypertension, COPD, and anxiety (e.g. panic) among others (see references below). Proper breathing can also increase cognitive performance and reduce the body’s reaction to stressors. Many studies have shown that the reasons therapeutic breathing may be so effective are that it increases oxygen saturation in your cells, increases lung function, increases brain waves associated with a relaxed but alert state, reduces inflammation and heart rate and creates balance in your autonomic nervous system and increases heart rate variability as well as the activity the bodily system that is associated with relaxation and bodily restoration called the parasympathetic nervous system (see references below). This may be why the majority of all mind-body interventions such as meditation, yoga (pranayama), and Qigong include slow therapeutic breathing as a central if not the central component of the practice. This is also true of general stress management and biofeedback interventions.

Andrew Weil, one of the leading integrative health figures today, highlights the power of breathing by stating: “Of all the techniques that I have investigated for reducing stress and increasing relaxation, it is breath work that I have found to be the most time-efficient, the most cost-efficient and the one that most promotes increased wellness and optimal health. In addition, working with the breath can be a very powerful technique to center the mind, to help you work more effectively, to help you deal with all of the challenges that life throws in your way every day.”

Breathing properly is not as easy as it seems. Many people don’t know how to breathe slowly properly or get distracted during breathing exercises which limits the efficacy of the practice. Breathing at or slightly below six breaths per minute causes balance in the autonomic nervous system and maximally increases heart rate variability (one of the best predictors of overall mortality and overall cardiac functioning), but everyone has a different breathing rate that creates this balance. While only biofeedback can find your optimal rate, we can make a great estimation based on height because there is research to show that blood volume circulated in the cardiovascular system is dependent on height. Therefore, taller people have lower optimal breathing rates. Breathpacer takes these individual differences in height into account and automatically assigns people a therapeutic breathing rate. People also have the option of choosing their own breathing rates because many yoga and meditation practitioners use different styles of breathing or different rates may be most useful for different disorders. For example, lower breathing rates (about 2-3 breaths per minute) may be more effective in reducing blood pressure whereas 5-6 breaths per minute breathing may be more effective for other disorders like depression. There are other techniques such as alternate nostril breathing and breath holding which also have value. The length of a session may also alter the effects. For example, shorter session of 5 or 10 minutes seem to invigorate people putting them in a relaxed but alert state whereas longer sessions induce deep relaxation. There is still much research to be done in this area but it is clear that slow therapeutic breathing is a more powerful intervention than most drugs and has cascading effects across bodily mechanisms. Breathpacer is a tool to help people practice slow therapeutic breathing using the empirical research as a guide. It includes options such as both audio and visual breathing cues and an option to gradually reduce ones breathing rate for beginners. If you need more help learning to breathe therapeutically there are some great instructional Youtube videos by Al Lee and Don Campbell, the authors of Perfect Breathing. (Watch video)
