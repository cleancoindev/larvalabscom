Our first mobile game sold 1 copy

by Matt Hall on Larva Labs /

The first game we ever wrote for a phone was called “Gurk” and it earned us a grand total of $3.99. It was a J2ME role-playing game that we did way, way back in 2005. It took us around 3 months, but the file size of whole thing was only 63.4K. The limit for OTA (over the air) app distribution at the time was 63.7K. That’s graphics, code, icon, everything. It was written in Java which was a language never designed to be size efficient, so there were all sorts of dumb tricks you’d do to reduce the size like put everything into one or two classes and reuse variables. Coming from enterprise Java web development where the XML config files to save a username and password were about 22 megs this was a big change, but it was fun too.

For example, late in the process of us writing it we realized we needed some kind of map for the player. The problem was there was no space left. John figured he needed about 500 bytes to track and display the map and we just didn’t have it. After taking a hard look at what we did have, the tough decision was made to remove the ability to name your character. This doomed every player to assume the role of “Sir Rugnar.” Important hindsight: If you’re going to make a name mandatory you should think long and hard before going with “Sir Rugnar.”

The best thing about the game was that it had a pretty big world, and compared to the games from that era it was huge. We had one really neat trick where we’d randomly generate a thousand dungeon maps on our laptop, then pick a bunch we liked and just write down the random seed. So the game contained the map generator, and a list of random seeds for each dungeon. Much smaller than actually storing the maps for all the dungeons. John even wrote a light ray algorithm so when you’re walking through the dungeon we only reveal the parts that your torch would have shown – spooky!

Another cool thing was how the character graphics at this size seem so abstract when zoomed in, but at their normal size your brain pulls all kinds of tricks to make it look like a little person.

The eventual downfall of the game though was the harsh realities of J2ME distribution. I remember we were at JavaOne 2005 and had printed out a hundred Gurk promo sheets to hand out to phone manufacturers. Needless to say they weren’t too interested in an old school 8-bit RPG with Ogres and square shorelines. One large handset manufacturer wanted us to pay $5,000 just to join some developer site and have the chance of being considered for distribution. The contrast with today’s direct to consumer app store model is striking and amazing.

Once we gave up and started giving the game away for free on our website, we actually received a lot of emails from people that loved the game. Probably if there had been a real app store back then we could have reached a few thousand people interested in playing an 8-bit style RPG on their phone. As it was we sold 1 copy through Handango for $3.99, far short of whatever payout threshold they had in place so our actual take was exactly zero. There’s a photo around somewhere of the Larva Labs 2005 holiday party which consists of me and John at an outback steakhouse splitting a Bloomin’ Onion for tax purposes. Does that even make sense? We also didn’t have an accountant then.

The big upside to this story is we met the developer relations people from Danger at that same JavaOne. While they didn’t want anything to do with Gurk they convinced us to write something specific to Sidekick. Because they did have a fairly modern app store in place our first app for Sidekick led to almost 40 more. It’s probably worth a separate post, but the Sidekick was so ahead of it’s time in so many ways it’s hard to believe in retrospect. An always connected full keyboard handset with push email, web browser, persistent IM and modern app store back in 2004 is pretty amazing.

If you happen to have a J2ME phone, or can figure out how to get it running in an emulator, here’s Gurk. If people show enough interest in the comments or on Twitter we might even be convinced to get it running on Android. We still have a soft spot for good ol’ Sir Rugnar.
